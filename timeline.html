A Critical History of
Artificial Intelligence

From the Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction to the White House robot demonstration of 2026 — a history of artificial intelligence situated within the histories of science, capital, ecology, and the democratic governance of technology.

This timeline traces the history of artificial intelligence — cutting through the hype to examine what the technology actually is, where it came from, and what is at stake. It draws on political economy, philosophy of technology, and education scholarship.

1750–1900Disciplinary Society

Surveillance, enclosure, normalization. The factory, prison, school, and hospital. Panopticon logic.

1900–1970Biopower / Biopolitics

Administration of populations — health, reproduction, productivity. Power over life itself.

1970–PresentControl Society

"One is never finished with anything" (Deleuze, 1990). Enclosures dissolve into continuous modulation — no walls, no timetable, just tracking. "Whether animal in a reserve or human in a corporation, as with an electronic collar." Zuboff: behavioral data as the mechanism. Saltman: the same logic in the AI classroom.

Pre-1771Pre-Capitalist Formations

Land, labor, and money not yet subject to market logic. Production embedded in social obligation and custom.

1771–1873First & Second Surges: Mechanization & Steam

Perez's first two surges. Financial capital begins decoupling from productive capacity. Polanyi's double movement: market expansion tears at social fabric; organized labor is the counter-movement.

1875–1974Steel, Electricity & the Fordist Golden Age

Third and Fourth Technological Revolutions (Perez). The Keynesian deployment phase — welfare state, union contract. Labor's historic high-water mark.

1971–PresentFifth Surge & Beyond: ICT, Surveillance Capitalism, and AI

Perez's Fifth Technological Revolution: dot-com frenzy, crash, deployment as platform economy. Zuboff: behavioral data as the new accumulation logic. Polanyi's double movement: market expansion into cognitive and creative labor produces organized counter-movements.

AI as a new surge: applying Perez's framework, AI investment displays the frenzy signature — capital decoupled from productive capacity, returns unproven. Goodlad and Stone: "no certainty about the economic impact of AI and no rising prosperity for anyone outside the elite echelons of power."

Pre-IndustrialCraft & Embedded Savoir-Faire

Simondon: technical objects are "abstract" — singular, tied to the craftsperson's milieu. Human and tool co-individuate. Technical objects address the world "point by point and step by step" — they can never reconstruct the original wholeness of the human-world relation (Simondon, 1958/2011, p. 421). Ellul: technique is local, plural, embedded in culture.

IndustrializationFirst Proletarianization: Loss of Savoir-Faire

The machine externalizes the worker's bodily knowledge. Simondon: the rise of the machine tool removes the skilled worker's capacity to differentiate their labor — a "loss of individuation." Stiegler: the worker is separated not merely from the means of production but from the knowledge that constituted their dignity. Ellul: technique becomes autonomous and self-justifying — efficiency its own moral order.

Mass FordismSecond Proletarianization: Loss of Savoir-Vivre

Consumer capitalism replaces savoir-vivre — knowledge of how to live — with the behavioural prescriptions produced by marketing. Stiegler: proletarianization is not poverty but the loss of knowledge. Ellul: technique colonizes leisure, desire, and aesthetics.

Digital / AI EraThird Proletarianization: Loss of Savoir-Conceptualiser

Conceptual knowledge finds itself ruined — "proletarianizing the intellectuals." Stiegler: the successive epochs of grammatization have extended proletarianization to "all areas of understanding and finally reason." The technology is a pharmakon — requiring both a toxicology and a therapeutics. Cope and Kalantzis: human and machine intelligence as complementary, not substitutive.

Industrial EraEcocide as Systemic Logic

Saltman (2024): ecocide names the systematic destruction of ecosystems as a consequence of capitalist production — not an accident of it. Capital externalizes ecological costs onto commons it cannot be held accountable for.

20th CenturyDemocratic Regulation as Counter-Movement

Saltman (2024): the same logic that privatizes public education privatizes the atmosphere. Goodlad and Stone (2024): corporate and state actors treat ecological costs as externalities while claiming technology will solve the crisis they produce.

Digital / AI EraThe Entropocene

Stiegler: capitalism produces entropy on a planetary scale — the Entropocene. Goodlad and Stone (2024): AI energy demands are "enormous and growing — and are treated as trade secrets by tech companies." The Neganthropocene is Stiegler's name for the alternative.

Phase I · Deep Time — Non-Linear Scale
66 Ma
Ecocide
Cretaceous-Paleogene Extinction
An asteroid impact eliminates ~75% of species, restructures the biosphere, and creates conditions for mammalian cognitive expansion.
▼

The K-Pg extinction event opens this timeline at geological scale. The impact that ended the Mesozoic eliminated approximately 75% of species, restructured the biosphere over millions of years, and created the ecological conditions within which mammalian cognitive complexity eventually emerged. Beginning here frames the timeline's ecological thread: the costs of planetary-scale disruption are real, cumulative, and unevenly distributed. That thread runs from this node through Love Canal, and the 2024 climate data to the projected consequences of unmitigated AI energy demand.

Citation: General scholarly knowledge.
~1M BCE
Co-Individuation
Fire — The First Technical Transformation
Controlled use of fire restructures human cognition, social organization, diet, and the relationship between the human body and its environment.
▼

Fire is the first moment in this timeline where a technical capability and human capacity mutually reshape each other at civilizational scale. Cooking changes the human gut and brain; the social gathering around fire restructures collective life and the transmission of knowledge across generations. Simondon's framework — that human beings and their technical objects co-constitute each other through an ongoing associated milieu — has its earliest legible instance here. The co-individuation thread begins at this node and runs through writing, the printing press, the steam engine, cybernetics, and the internet to the present question of what generative AI is doing to human cognitive capacity.

Citation: General scholarly knowledge. Theoretical framing: Simondon, G., Mellamphy, N., Mellamphy, D., & Mellamphy, N. B. (2011). On the mode of existence of technical objects. Deleuze Studies, 5(3), 407–424.
~3200 BCE
Co-Individuation
Writing — Memory Externalized
The invention of writing externalizes human memory into durable inscription, transforming collective knowledge and establishing the original pharmakon — technology as both poison and cure.
▼

Writing is the original pharmakon. Plato argued in the Phaedrus that writing destroys memory and weakens the mind. The same anxiety about technology displacing human capacity recurs at every subsequent threshold — printing press, calculator, internet, AI. Goodlad and Stone (2024) invoke Derrida's reading of Plato's pharmacy to frame AI as the latest instance of this ancient ambivalence. Stiegler's concept of tertiary retention — externalized memory that both enables and disables — begins here.

1440
Co-IndividuationCapital & Labor
The Printing Press — Knowledge Democratized, Power Restructured
Gutenberg's movable type press transforms the reproduction of knowledge, breaks the Church's information monopoly, and establishes the first moment where capital takes a direct interest in controlling the means of knowledge production.
▼

The printing press transformed the reproduction and distribution of knowledge at civilizational scale — enabling the Reformation, the Scientific Revolution, new forms of collective political life, and new forms of propaganda and ideological control simultaneously. Stiegler's concept of transindividuation — the technical mediation of collective memory and meaning — finds one of its earliest large-scale expressions here. The printing press is also the first moment where capital takes a direct and sustained interest in controlling the means of knowledge production. That dynamic recurs with every subsequent information technology: telegraph, radio, television, internet, and now AI, each time under conditions shaped by the concentration of ownership over the infrastructure.

1646–1716
Co-Individuation
Leibniz and the Logic of Binary
Gottfried Leibniz formalizes binary arithmetic and declares "Let us calculate" — the proposition that all worldly complexity can be represented mathematically and reasoned computationally, drawing inspiration from the Chinese I Ching.
▼

Cope and Kalantzis (2022) identify Leibniz as "the earliest exponent of the modern idea of computability," noting he drew inspiration from the Chinese I Ching — the work of Shao Yong (1012–1077). Leibniz also published the chain rule essential to modern deep learning and described binary computer principles governed by punch cards (1679). The universalizing ambition in "Let us calculate" — that reason itself can be mechanized — is the philosophical seed of every subsequent claim about "artificial intelligence." Cope and Kalantzis argue that this ambition carries within it a persistent tension: their analysis of the six transpositions shows that statistical quantification (the sixth) has been privileged over the other five modes of meaning — reference, property, context, agency, and media — in ways that have shaped what computing has been asked to do and for whom.

Citation: Cope, B., & Kalantzis, M. (2022). Artificial intelligence in the long view. Discover Artificial Intelligence, 2, Article 13. https://doi.org/10.1007/s44163-022-00029-1
1750s–1830s
Capital & LaborEcocide
Enclosure of the Commons
The systematic enclosure of shared agricultural land dispossesses peasant communities, creates the landless wage-labor force industrial capitalism requires, and establishes the template for every subsequent act of capital enclosure.
▼

Marx names this "primitive accumulation" — the original act of separation that creates the conditions for capitalist production. Polanyi describes it as the beginning of the "great transformation": the conversion of land, labor, and money into fictitious commodities, subjected to market logic for the first time. Perez's (2003) framework of technological surges builds on this foundation — each subsequent surge requires a fresh supply of labor separated from prior means of subsistence or production. The enclosure of the agricultural commons is the template: the enclosure of craft knowledge by the factory, of behavioral data by platforms, and of public education by EdTech are structural repetitions of the same logic across different historical conditions.

~1790–1830
Capital & LaborCo-IndividuationPower & Control
The Factory Floor — Entering the Disciplinary Enclosure
Vagrancy laws criminalize displacement, compelling dispossessed peasants into factory wage labor. The body is timed, surveilled, and normalized to the machine's rhythm. Stiegler's first proletarianization — the loss of savoir-faire — begins here.
▼

Polanyi (1944) documents that the self-regulating market required not just the enclosure of land but the legal compulsion of labor: vagrancy laws made displacement a criminal condition, channeling the dispossessed into factories on capital's terms. Foucault's disciplinary logic is immediately legible on the factory floor — the body timed, ranked, and normalized to industrial rhythm. This is where Stiegler's first proletarianization begins in practice: the craftsperson enters the factory as an operative. The knowledge that constituted their skill begins its transfer into the machine. Taylor will systematize that transfer; Ford will complete it.

1784
Co-Individuation
Bolton & Watt Steam Engine — The First Non-Trivial Machine
The steam engine's governor — spinning weights that self-regulate steam input — is the first practical feedback machine: a rudimentary learning system and the mechanical precursor to cybernetics.
▼

Cope and Kalantzis (2022) cite Von Foerster's distinction between "trivial machines" (acting on command) and "non-trivial machines" (changing internal state based on new input and previous state). The Bolton & Watt governor is the first industrial non-trivial machine: "When the machine runs too fast, the balls spin wide and reduce the steam input, and when it runs too slow, they fall inward, thus increasing the input of steam." This is Wiener's cybernetics avant la lettre — the feedback loop as the foundational logic of what will eventually be called "machine learning." The industrial machine also restructures the human body: workers become appendages of a rhythm they did not set.

Citation: Cope, B., & Kalantzis, M. (2022). Artificial intelligence in the long view. Discover Artificial Intelligence, 2, Article 13. https://doi.org/10.1007/s44163-022-00029-1. Theoretical framing: Simondon, G. et al. (2011). On the mode of existence of technical objects. Deleuze Studies, 5(3), 407–424.
1843
Co-Individuation
Lovelace & Babbage — The First Vision of Programmable Machines
Ada Lovelace's notes on Babbage's Analytical Engine establish the founding insight that machines could process intelligible meaning — that the Jacquard loom's punch-card logic could be extended to computation itself.
▼

Cope and Kalantzis (2022) cite Lovelace's recognition that the punched-card programmability of the Jacquard loom could be applied to Babbage's calculating machine. Lovelace concluded it could "weave algebraical patterns just as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves." The Jacquard loom is itself a co-individuation node: the weaver's pattern knowledge encoded in physical media, externalized from the craftsperson's hands into punch cards. Lovelace's conceptual leap connects programmability with representations of the natural world — computing becomes, in principle, a general-purpose tool for meaning-making, not merely arithmetic.

Citation: Cope, B., & Kalantzis, M. (2022). Artificial intelligence in the long view. Discover Artificial Intelligence, 2, Article 13 [citing Lovelace, A. A. (1843). Sketch of the analytical engine. In Scientific Memoirs (pp. 666–731). R. and J. E. Taylor.]
1840s
Capital & Labor
Railway Mania & Crash — The Perezian Template
The speculative frenzy of the 1840s railway boom and its catastrophic collapse establishes the recurring pattern — technological surge, financial decoupling, crash — that will repeat through every subsequent technology revolution including AI.
▼

Perez (2003) identifies the railway mania of the 1840s as the paradigmatic case of her "frenzy" phase: financial capital decouples entirely from productive capacity, speculation becomes self-referential, and the inevitable crash leaves behind useful infrastructure but destroys paper wealth. This pattern — installation, frenzy, crash, deployment — repeats through the dot-com bubble and, arguably, the current AI investment frenzy. Marx writes Capital in the aftermath of this crash, anatomizing the commodity form and surplus value. The railway mania is also the first moment when financial capital explicitly drives technological development rather than following it — the pattern that defines AI investment today.

Citation: Perez, C. (2003). Technological revolutions and financial capital (Repr). Elgar.
Phase II · Modern History — Consistent Spacing
1911–1913
Capital & LaborCo-Individuation
Taylor & Ford — Capturing the Worker's Gesture
Taylor's Principles of Scientific Management (1911) decomposes craft knowledge into measurable protocols; Ford's assembly line (1913) embeds those protocols in machinery, making the knowledge permanently redundant.
▼

Cope and Kalantzis (2022) document that "Henry Ford created a production line to manufacture his Model T motor car, requiring eight thousand finely differentiated steps. Engineers like Frederick Winslow Taylor designed processes that optimized the productivity of the relations between bodies and machines." This is Stiegler's first proletarianization — the loss of savoir-faire — made historically concrete. The worker is separated not only from the means of production (Marx) but from the knowledge that constituted their dignity. Saltman (2020) draws an explicit connection forward: biometric pedagogy in schools is "radically regressive, dredging up early twentieth-century models of scientific management and Taylorism" — the decomposition and measurement logic of Taylor applied to student attention and behavior rather than worker output.

Citation: Cope, B., & Kalantzis, M. (2022). Artificial intelligence in the long view. Discover Artificial Intelligence, 2, Article 13; Saltman, K. J. (2020). Artificial intelligence and the technological turn of public education privatization. London Review of Education, 18(2), 196–208.
1936
Co-Individuation
Turing — On Computable Numbers
Alan Turing formalizes what it means for a procedure to be mechanical, establishing the theoretical architecture of all modern computation and positing the universal machine capable of simulating any other machine.
▼

Cope and Kalantzis (2022) cite Turing's 1936 paper as establishing foundational computability theory. The universal Turing machine — a machine that can simulate any other machine given the right program — is the conceptual basis for the stored-program computer and for the proposition that intelligence itself might be mechanizable. Cope and Kalantzis note that the later McCulloch-Pitts neuron model (1943) inspired Von Neumann's computer architecture and became "the source of persistent overblown claims about AI, since it conflated machine intelligence with human intelligence despite McCulloch and Pitts themselves cautioning that the physiological brain was far more than this." The brain/computer analogy has since generated productive research and significant overstatement in equal measure.

Citation: Cope, B., & Kalantzis, M. (2022). Artificial intelligence in the long view. Discover Artificial Intelligence, 2, Article 13 [citing Turing, A. M. (1936). On computable numbers. Proceedings of the London Mathematical Society, 42(2), 230–265.]
1948–1950
Co-Individuation
The Wiener / Turing Fork — The Road Not Taken
Norbert Wiener's Cybernetics frames intelligence as recursive feedback between humans and machines — complementarity. Turing's imitation game frames it as substitution. The Turing framing wins, narrowing the field for seven decades.
▼

Cope and Kalantzis (2022) identify this as the decisive fork: "Wiener toward human-machine complementarity, Turing toward substitution and the brain/computer parallel." Wiener's cybernetics offered a framework where human and machine intelligence are radically different and mutually constitutive — a feedback relationship rather than a competition. Turing asked "Can machines think?" and framed the answer as a substitution test. Every subsequent "AI benchmark" — chess, Go, image recognition, language generation — follows Turing's substitution logic. The historical consequence is significant: a field oriented toward complementarity would have asked different research questions, developed different architectures, and produced different relationships to human labor and capacity. Cope and Kalantzis argue for returning to the cybernetic tradition — not the terminology, but the underlying relational framework.

Citation: Cope, B., & Kalantzis, M. (2022). Artificial intelligence in the long view. Discover Artificial Intelligence, 2, Article 13 [citing Turing, A. M. (1950). Computing machinery and intelligence. Mind, 59(236), 433–460; Wiener, N. (1948). Cybernetics. MIT Press.]
1956
Co-IndividuationCapital & Labor
Dartmouth — "Artificial Intelligence" Named and Narrowed
John McCarthy coins "artificial intelligence" at a small Dartmouth workshop partly to escape association with Wiener's cybernetics. The naming constitutes AI as a discrete, fundable research program — a capital event as much as a scientific one.
▼

The original 1955 Dartmouth proposal — authored by McCarthy, Minsky, Rochester, and Shannon — proceeds "on the basis of the conjecture that every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it." Cope and Kalantzis (2022) document that McCarthy "deliberately coined the term 'artificial intelligence' to escape association with cybernetics." What is lost in the naming is Wiener's recursive feedback and complementarity. What is gained is a field that can attract grants, publish papers, and eventually secure venture capital. The substitution logic is now institutionalized: intelligence as a human property that machines can replicate, not a relational property that emerges between humans and machines.

Citation: McCarthy, J., Minsky, M. L., Rochester, N., & Shannon, C. E. (2006). A proposal for the Dartmouth summer research project on artificial intelligence, August 31, 1955. AI Magazine, 27(4), 12–14; Cope, B., & Kalantzis, M. (2022). Artificial intelligence in the long view. Discover Artificial Intelligence, 2, Article 13.
1957–1969
Co-IndividuationCapital & Labor
The Perceptron & the First "AI Winter" — Capital Withdraws
Rosenblatt's Perceptron (1957) is the first neural network hardware. Minsky and Papert's Perceptrons (1969) demonstrates limits of shallow networks, triggering a funding withdrawal. The "winter" is a capital allocation decision, not a scientific failure.
▼

LeCun (2019) documents that Bell Labs built increasingly capable neural net chips through the 1960s. The "winter" framing deserves scrutiny — hence the scare quotes. The withdrawal of government and corporate funding was not primarily caused by technical failure; the underlying algorithms were valid. LeCun's analysis identifies hardware limitations and data scarcity — not algorithmic inadequacy — as the actual constraints on progress. The "winter" reflects a cycle of investment expectation and disappointment that Perez identifies in every technological surge: capital commits on the basis of projected timelines that the science cannot reliably meet, then withdraws when those projections are not fulfilled. The field continued developing; the funding did not.

Citation: LeCun, Y. (2019). Deep learning hardware: Past, present, and future. 2019 IEEE ISSCC Digest, pp. 12–19 [citing Rosenblatt, F. (1957). The perceptron, a perceiving and recognizing automaton. Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory; Minsky, M., & Papert, S. A. (1969). Perceptrons. MIT Press.]
1962–1970
Ecocide
EPA Founded — Democratic Regulation Responds to Industrial Destruction
The National Environmental Policy Act (1970) establishes the Environmental Protection Agency — the first major democratic regulatory response to industrial capital's systematic externalizing of ecological costs onto the commons.
▼

Industrial pesticides and chemical dumping had systematically destroyed ecosystems throughout the postwar Fordist boom — exposing the ecological underside of prosperity. Nixon signed the National Environmental Policy Act (1970) and established the Environmental Protection Agency in direct response to organized public pressure. In Polanyi's terms, this is the double movement at institutional scale: market expansion destroys the ecological commons; organized citizens and democratic institutions respond with regulatory power. The EPA is the historical template for the closing projection this timeline poses about AI — the same structural question, whether democratic institutions can act before ecological costs are permanently externalized, was answered in 1970. The question is whether it can be answered again.

Citation: National Environmental Policy Act, Pub. L. No. 91-190, 83 Stat. 852 (1970). [Primary legislation, public record.]
1978
EcocideCapital & Labor
Love Canal — Industrial Capital's Toxic Residue
President Carter declares a federal emergency at Love Canal — the first man-made environmental disaster to receive such a designation — after Hooker Chemical's 21,000 tons of buried toxic waste poisons a residential neighborhood for decades.
▼

On August 7, 1978, Love Canal became the first human-caused environmental disaster to receive a federal emergency designation. Hooker Chemical had buried 21,000 tons of hazardous waste — then sold the land to a school board for $1 with a liability waiver. Residents experienced miscarriages, cancers, and birth defects for decades. Love Canal demonstrates the logic of capitalist externalization: profits are privatized; costs — ecological, bodily, generational — are socialized onto the most vulnerable communities. The disaster prompted the Superfund legislation (CERCLA, 1980). This node sits inside Perez's Fourth Surge deployment phase — the Fordist golden age exporting its toxins into residential neighborhoods while producing consumer abundance for others.

Citation: Silverman, G. (1989). Love Canal: A retrospective. Environmental Reporter, 20(20–2), 835–850; Carter, J. (1978, August 7). Federal emergency declaration, Love Canal [Executive Order]; Gensburg, L. J., et al. (2009). Mortality among former Love Canal residents. Environmental Health Perspectives, 117(2), 209–216.
1986
Co-Individuation
Backpropagation — Neural Networks Become Trainable
Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams popularize backpropagation, enabling multilayer neural networks to learn from data at scale. Bell Labs applies the method commercially within two years.
▼

LeCun (2019) documents the algorithmic foundation and its rapid commercial application: LeCun joined Bell Labs in 1988 and applied convolutional neural networks to handwritten zip code recognition; by the late 1990s the system was reading 10–20% of all US bank checks — the first major commercial deployment of deep learning. The priority dispute around backpropagation — Schmidhuber's (2022) documentation that Linnainmaa's earlier work went uncited by Rumelhart et al. — raises broader questions about how attribution works in computational science: how findings are institutionalized, which lineages become canonical, and how the history of a technology gets written in relation to its subsequent commercial success.

Citation: LeCun, Y. (2019). Deep learning hardware. 2019 IEEE ISSCC Digest, pp. 12–19 [citing Rumelhart, D. E., Hinton, G. E., & Williams, R. J. (1986). Learning representations by back-propagating errors. Nature, 323, 533–536.]; Schmidhuber, J. (2022). Annotated history of modern AI and deep learning. IDSIA Technical Report.
1987–2005
Co-IndividuationCapital & Labor
Second "AI Winter" — Capital Withdraws Again; Practitioners Keep Building
Academic and government capital withdraws from AI research as promised capabilities fail to materialize on schedule. In Silicon Valley, practitioners build the distributed data infrastructure that makes the next AI surge possible.
▼

LeCun (2019) identifies the constraint as hardware and data availability, not algorithmic failure. But the standard narrative — that AI "stalled" — obscures what was actually happening in industry. During this same period, practitioners at San Francisco firms including Splunk were pioneering the processing of massive, unstructured machine datasets. Engineers were repurposing NVIDIA GPUs for parallel computation. Apache Hadoop democratized the distributed architecture published in Google's MapReduce papers. The "winter" is a capital and academic story. The infrastructure story runs continuously underneath it. ChatGPT did not emerge from a breakthrough — it emerged from two decades of unglamorous industrial data engineering, largely invisible to the historians who would later write the "AI revolution" narrative. (Eyewitness account, Silicon Valley practitioner, 2005–2008.)

Citation: LeCun, Y. (2019). Deep learning hardware. 2019 IEEE ISSCC, pp. 12–19; Schmidhuber, J. (2022). Annotated history of modern AI. IDSIA. Practitioner eyewitness account (Silicon Valley, 2005–2008).
1995–2002
Capital & Labor
The Dot-com Frenzy & Crash — Perez's Fifth Surge
The NASDAQ bubble inflates on speculation about the internet's transformative potential, then collapses, destroying trillions in paper wealth while leaving behind the infrastructure that enables the surveillance economy.
▼

Perez (2003) identifies the dot-com frenzy as the canonical installation-phase speculative bubble of the Fifth Technological Revolution. Financial capital decouples entirely from productive capacity. The crash leaves behind the deployment infrastructure: fiber optic cables, server farms, internet protocols, and accumulated engineering expertise. Goodlad and Stone (2024) document what fills this infrastructure in the deployment phase: not the visionary startups of the frenzy, but tech companies that became "surveillance empires: powerful purveyors of data and advertising." The crash is the hinge between the internet as commons and the internet as extraction machine — exactly the transition Polanyi's double movement predicts and exactly the transition that sets conditions for AI's current political economy.

Citation: Perez, C. (2002). Technological revolutions and financial capital. Edward Elgar; Goodlad, L. M. E., & Stone, M. (2024). Beyond Chatbot-K. Critical AI, 2(1). https://doi.org/10.1215/2834703X-11205147
2003–2004
Capital & LaborPower & Control
Surveillance Capitalism — Behavioral Data as Raw Material
Google's AdWords system establishes behavioral data as the raw material of a new accumulation logic, transforming the internet from a communication medium into an extraction machine and inaugurating what Zuboff calls "surveillance capitalism."
▼

Goodlad and Stone (2024) document that tech companies turned into "powerful purveyors of data and advertising which established them among the world's largest and most profitable corporations (see, e.g., Zuboff 2018)." Zuboff's (2020) concept of surveillance capitalism names the specific mechanism: behavioral data — the byproduct of human activity online — is extracted, processed into predictive products, and sold to advertisers seeking to modify behavior at scale. Goodlad and Stone identify "data positivism" — the belief that statistical pattern-matching at scale constitutes knowledge — as the accompanying ideological formation. This belief, they argue, narrows the framework for the production and evaluation of knowledge while legitimating a political economy that amplifies surveillance and the concentration of computational resources.

Citation: Goodlad, L. M. E., & Stone, M. (2024). Beyond Chatbot-K. Critical AI, 2(1). https://doi.org/10.1215/2834703X-11205147; Zuboff, S. (2018). The age of surveillance capitalism. PublicAffairs.
2005–2010
Co-Individuation
Sun, Hadoop, Splunk, CUDA — The Industrial Data Infrastructure "AI" Forgot
Silicon Valley practitioners — drawing on Sun's network philosophy, Google's published MapReduce architecture, Splunk's unstructured data tools, and NVIDIA's repurposed GPUs — build the distributed infrastructure that makes modern AI possible.
▼

Sun Microsystems' foundational philosophy — "The Network is the Computer" — established distributed computing as the dominant paradigm. Apache Hadoop (2006) democratized the architecture published in Google's MapReduce papers, allowing organizations without Google's resources to process data at comparable scale. Splunk pioneered the legibility of unstructured machine data — log files, event streams, telemetry — at industrial scale. NVIDIA's CUDA (2007) was adopted as a tactical workaround: engineers repurposed graphics processing units for the parallel mathematical operations required to train models on large datasets. Standard histories of AI tend to center the academic deep learning renaissance of 2012 and the major laboratory research programs. This practitioner-driven, distributed infrastructure story — developed in the industrial sector throughout the supposed "winter" — is less visible in those accounts but equally consequential for what became possible. (Eyewitness account, Silicon Valley practitioner, 2005–2008.)

Citation: LeCun, Y. (2019). Deep learning hardware. 2019 IEEE ISSCC, pp. 12–19; Schmidhuber, J. (2022). Annotated history of modern AI. IDSIA. Practitioner eyewitness account (Silicon Valley, 2005–2008).
2015–Present
Capital & LaborPower & Control
AI Education Privatization — The Child as Data Engine
For-profit AI education companies shift ownership of curriculum from teachers and communities to corporations, converting student behavior into commodified data while claiming to "personalize" learning.
▼

Saltman (2020) documents how for-profit AI education companies — including Chan Zuckerberg Initiative's Summit platform, built by Facebook engineers — are attempting to shift ownership and control of curriculum from teachers and communities to investors and corporations. The attempt involves suppressing teacher wages, transferring political control over pedagogy to private actors, and framing students' behavioral data as a commercial asset. Biometric pedagogy devices — "radically regressive, dredging up early twentieth-century models of scientific management and Taylorism" — measure physical responses to lessons as proxies for learning. Saltman is clear that these efforts have largely failed to capture public education, which remains substantially under democratic control. What he documents is the logic driving repeated attempts: the same Bill Gates-backed venture philanthropy model that has tried and failed through charter schools, standardized testing, and corporate management is now returning through AI. The pattern matters because each iteration requires organized resistance from educators, communities, and democratic institutions to beat back — and the AI iteration is the most technically sophisticated yet.

▼

The Oligarchy Problem

Two threads run separately through this timeline — Capital & Labor, and Power & Control — and arrive at the same point. The mechanisms of state power and the logic of capital accumulation, which Foucault and Perez theorize independently, converge in the present moment: the same corporations hold defense contracts, shape educational policy, and control the infrastructure of public discourse. The question is whether democratic institutions and organized labor will act before those two threads lock permanently into one.

Does the AI Investment Cycle Follow the Perezian Pattern?
Goodlad and Stone document that more than a year into the commercial phase of generative AI there is still no certainty about its economic impact. If Perez's frenzy-crash framework applies, what conditions would govern the deployment phase that follows?
▼

Goodlad and Stone (2024) note that "more than a year into the commercial phase of this supposed revolution, there is still no certainty about the economic impact of AI and no rising prosperity for anyone outside the elite echelons of power." Perez's (2003) framework identifies the frenzy phase as necessarily preceding a correction: financial capital, having decoupled from productive capacity, returns to equilibrium through a crash that redistributes assets and establishes the regulatory and institutional conditions for the deployment phase. Whether the current AI investment cycle conforms to this pattern — and if so, what the conditions of its deployment phase would be — are open empirical questions. The degree to which AI differs from prior cycles in asset concentration, geopolitical entanglement, and integration into critical public infrastructure may affect how those questions are answered.

Will Educators, Labor, and Democratic Institutions Assert Public Control Over AI?
Selwyn asks directly whether teachers will refuse to be replaced by robots — and what conditions would need to exist for democratic institutions and organized labor to shape AI's role in education rather than simply absorb it.
▼

Selwyn (2019) poses the question directly: Should Robots Replace Teachers? — examining not only whether AI can perform teaching functions but whether it should, and on whose terms. Saltman (2020) documents how AI education privatization has proceeded largely without the consent of teachers, students, parents, or communities, transferring democratic control over curriculum and pedagogy to investors and corporations. The resistance both scholars call for is grounded in a long tradition of democratic education that holds the human relationship between teacher and student to be the irreducible core of learning — not a variable to be optimized, not a cost to be automated away, but the condition under which knowledge becomes meaningful rather than merely delivered. The WGA/SAG-AFTRA strikes of 2023 represent the first instance of organized labor explicitly contesting AI's displacement of creative and cognitive work. Whether educators follow that precedent, and whether democratic regulatory institutions act before the infrastructure of public education is permanently transferred to private capital, are the central questions this timeline has been building toward. Habermas's analysis of the structural transformation of the public sphere is directly relevant: democratic legitimacy requires a communicative public sphere not colonized by systemic power. When the infrastructure of education, discourse, and cognitive labor is concentrated in a small number of private actors, it is not only policy that is at stake — it is the structural conditions for democratic life itself.
